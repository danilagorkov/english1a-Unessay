FAILURE IS ESSENTIAL TO WRITING

We try to avoid failure at all costs and think that we are the only ones who ever give up. And what's worse, we're afraid we'll never be able to punch back. However, many people realize over time that their biggest struggles have become their most important life lessons.

In the research titled “‘Fail better’: Reconsidering the role of struggle and failure in academic writing development in higher education” Amanda French argues that the process of failure is undervalued in modern society. She believes that failure always teaches you to analyze. If you do not want to repeat your mistakes, you will have to understand why they happened. Similarly when reading scholarly text many students fail because they are unable to comprehend or analyze the text for important details and ideas. Without comprehension the student does not possess the ability to transfer his knowledge on paper and collaborate with other students.

Can failure lead to success? According to the theory of Manu Kapur this process revolves around 4 stages.

  • in order to solve a task the person requires the use of previously acquired knowledge and skills
  • search for solutions sparks excitement, curiosity, and interest
  • students realize what information they have and try to apply it
  • they fail multiple times before coming up with a unique solution

The main thing is to get a negative experience with maximum benefit. You must be able to analyze each of your failures. Be prepared for failures, because they are the predictors of success. And the more stunning and serious the failure, the stronger and louder your success will be in the future.

At first glance, the idea that failures come from working effectively seems absurd. Of course, every mistake made gives a person experience, but how does it manifest itself in motivation?

"When asked if failure had and will typically lead to growth and improvements personally and/or in their daily career roles on the member checking survey, 69 of the 78 respondents (88%) agreed."(Amber Simpson and Adam Maltese , 1)

Utilizing interview data with professionals across STEM Amber Simpson and Adam Maltese were able to support their claim that failure leads to success. The majority of interviewed people reported that failure helped them to adjust their workflow and improve their motivation. Failure provided them with skills that helped them with further education and employment.
